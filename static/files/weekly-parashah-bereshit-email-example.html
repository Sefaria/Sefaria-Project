Shanah tovah, and welcome (back) to Sefaria’s Weekly Parashah Study Companion! This week is an exciting week for followers of the weekly Torah portion — Jewish communities around the world will celebrate Simchat Torah by reading the very last Torah portion, VeZot HaBerakhah, and then rolling their scrolls all the way back to the beginning, and starting again from Bereshit. Last week, we sent out an email about VeZot HaBerakhah (which you can read here, if you missed it.) Having read the very beginning of Parashat Bereshit (“in the beginning”) on Simchat Torah, we now settle into the new cycle in earnest this Shabbat by reading the full portion, which relates the stories of the creation of the world and the first generations of humanity.

וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים יְהִי אוֹר וַיְהִי־אוֹר׃

God said, “Let there be light”; and there was light.

Genesis 1:3

After God creates the earth and all its creatures, Parashat Bereshit tells how the first people, Adam and Eve, eat from the Tree of Knowledge and are banished from the Garden of Eden. The parashah continues with their elder son, Cain, being destined to a life of wandering after killing his brother, Abel. Finally, the Torah intersperses a genealogy with some mysterious hints of various familial stories and concludes with an ominous preview of next week’s parashah.

GOING DEEPER

A Divine Recalibration

God is called by several different names in the Torah. Why does the first mention of God use the divine name that Jewish tradition associates with the quality of strict judgement? Rashi, the renowned 11th-century northern French rabbi and commentator, suggests a lesson about God’s creative process and relationship with the created world. See Rashi on Genesis 1:1.

The First Births

Any baby born is an amazement, but imagine the surprise as a parent welcoming the first babies ever born. In his elucidation of Parashat Bereshit, 20th-century scholar Rabbi Adin Steinsaltz suggests how Cain and Abel each brought a new understanding of the world to Adam and Eve. See Steinsaltz on Genesis 4:2.

CONNECTING THE HAFTARAH

The haftarah (accompanying reading from Prophets) for Parashat Bereshit is from the book of Isaiah (42:5–21 in the Sephardic tradition and 42:5–43:10 in the Ashkenazic tradition). This passage of prophetic text parallels the description of creation in Genesis, with the first few verses using the image of God as creator to underscore the divine ability to strengthen and preserve the people of Israel — and why they should, therefore, sing God’s praises.

Questions for Reflection

Parashat Bereshit includes both the story of God creating the world and the story of the first act of human violence (Cain murdering Abel). What do you think we might learn from the close proximity of these two stories?

In this parashah, the text says that humans were created in the “image of God.” What do you think this means, and how might this idea shape our understanding of human dignity and worth?

According to Rashi (referenced above), God originally planned to create the world based solely on the attribute of justice, before having a change of heart. Why do you think God decided the world could not endure under justice alone?
