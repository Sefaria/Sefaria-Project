Silence

Judaism is often thought of as a sound-filled religion â€” God uses speech to utter the world into existence, and synagogues and study halls are often places of audible prayer and learning. Yet, silence has its place in the Jewish textual tradition. For example, in the Torah, the high priest Aaron reacts to the death of his two sons with silence, and Pirkei Avot (a tractate of the Mishnah filled with ethical maxims) praises silence as a "fence to wisdom." Sources throughout the centuries discuss the place and value of silence.

The Legal Implications of Silence

RESPONSA

Silence in a legal context is not automatically considered an admission of guilt or truth. The 13th-century responsa of Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg, known as the Maharam, explores the conditions under which silence can be interpreted in Jewish legal proceedings.

Teshuvot Maharam, Cremona Edition 261:1

Q. A pressed many claims against B. To one of his claims B made no reply. Is B's silence to be considered an admission of the truth of A's claim?

A. Silence is not considered admission even if the defendant kept silent at court, unless the claimant summoned his listeners to witness the fact that the defendant did not deny his claim, and the latter still failed to reply.
